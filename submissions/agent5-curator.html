II

Sheats-Goldstein Residence

John Lautner (American, 1911-1994)

Concrete, glass, coffered ceiling system, integrated furnishings

1961, with additions 1963 and 1989

10,000 sq. ft. living space; 3.5 acres site

About This Work

Considered among the most significant residential works of twentieth-century American architecture, the Sheats-Goldstein Residence exemplifies John Lautner's philosophy of organic modernism. The structure emerges from its hillside site in Beverly Crest, its triangulated concrete forms and expansive glass walls creating a seamless dialogue between interior space and the Los Angeles basin beyond. The coffered concrete ceiling system, a signature Lautner innovation, eliminates the need for traditional support columns while providing integrated climate control.

Edgar Tafel

American, 1912-2011

"Architecture is not about making buildings. It is about making places for people to live their lives. Every line drawn, every material chosen, must serve the human spirit."

Biography

Edgar Tafel joined Frank Lloyd Wright's Taliesin Fellowship in 1932 at age nineteen, becoming one of the most accomplished apprentices of his generation. He worked directly on Fallingwater (1935-1939), the Kaufmann family's residence that would become the most celebrated house of the twentieth century. After leaving Taliesin, Tafel established his own practice in New York City, where he designed residences, religious buildings, and institutional structures for nearly six decades.

His 1979 memoir "Apprentice to Genius: Years with Frank Lloyd Wright" remains an essential primary source on the Taliesin experience, offering intimate accounts of Wright's teaching methods, the daily life of the Fellowship, and the collaborative process behind Wright's greatest works. Tafel received numerous honors including the AIA New York Chapter Medal of Honor.
