Sheats-Goldstein Residence

by John Lautner

Perched on a Beverly Crest hillside, the Sheats-Goldstein Residence represents the apex of John Lautner's organic architecture philosophy. The structure emerges from the landscape like a geological formation, its coffered concrete ceiling flowing seamlessly into the surrounding terrain. Glass walls dissolve the boundary between interior and exterior, while the dramatic cantilevers create a sense of weightlessness. In 2016, owner James Goldstein bequeathed the property to LACMA, ensuring its preservation as a museum of architectural achievement.

JL

John Lautner

"Architecture should be the background for life."

John Edward Lautner (1911-1994) emerged from Frank Lloyd Wright's Taliesin Fellowship to become one of the most innovative architects of the twentieth century. Where Wright emphasized horizontal prairie lines, Lautner developed a more sculptural approach, treating each commission as a unique response to site, client, and the possibilities of structure.

His California residences, from the octagonal Chemosphere perched on a concrete pedestal to the cave-like Sheats-Goldstein, represent organic architecture evolved beyond its prairie origins. Lautner embraced dramatic cantilevers, sweeping curves, and the integration of interior and landscape that continues to influence contemporary practice.
